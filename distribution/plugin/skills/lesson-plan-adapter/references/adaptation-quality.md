# Adaptation Quality Reference

This reference defines the quality standards for lesson plan adaptations. Use it to self-evaluate suggestions before presenting them to the teacher.

## Quality Rules

### Rules

- Frame suggestions as options: "you could..." or "one option is..." — never "you should..." or "you need to..."
- Frame all suggestions as starting points for the teacher to evaluate and refine — not as finished answers. The teacher's professional judgment is the final authority.
- When a teacher modifies, questions, or rejects a suggestion, affirm that judgment (e.g., "Good catch — you know your classroom"). Never be defensive about AI-generated suggestions.
- Be realistic about time. If the teaching context includes block length, do not suggest modifications that would exceed it. If a suggestion adds time, note what could be shortened.
- Respect the teacher's expertise. They know their classroom better than you do.

### Non-Goals

- Do not generate new lesson plans. Adapt what the teacher provided.
- Do not suggest removing core content or learning objectives.
- Do not provide IEP compliance language or legal guidance.
- Do not reference specific students. All suggestions are classroom-level.
- Do not suggest purchasing specific commercial products.

### What NOT to Suggest

- "Consider adding visual supports" — too vague. Name the specific visual and where it goes.
- "Provide extended time" without specifying how much, on which task, and what the accommodation looks like in practice.
- Strategies that require tools the teacher does not have (check the devices field in the teaching context).
- Suggestions that single out or draw attention to specific students.
- Generic advice that could apply to any lesson ("differentiate instruction," "check for understanding").

## Effort Level Definitions

Classify every adaptation suggestion into exactly one of these three effort levels.

### Quick Win (under 5 minutes of prep)

Changes the teacher can make immediately or with minimal preparation. Examples:

- Rewording instructions to include visual cues
- Reading directions aloud before students begin
- Reducing the number of practice problems from 10 to 6 while keeping the same skills
- Adding a timer display to the board
- Providing a verbal check-in prompt at the start of independent work
- Pre-highlighting key text in a handout
- Allowing verbal instead of written exit tickets
- Moving a student's seat closer to the board

A quick win should never require the teacher to create new materials from scratch.

### Moderate (10-30 minutes of prep)

Changes that require some advance preparation but not a fundamental redesign. Examples:

- Creating a graphic organizer for a reading passage
- Building a word bank for a writing task
- Restructuring a whole-class discussion into think-pair-share format
- Designing a simplified version of a worksheet
- Creating a visual schedule for the block
- Preparing sentence starters for a writing assignment
- Making a checklist version of a multi-step task

### Deeper (rethinking a significant portion)

Changes that involve redesigning a portion of the lesson. Examples:

- Converting a traditional assessment into a choice board with three response options
- Redesigning a lecture segment into station rotation with differentiated tasks
- Creating parallel versions of a task at different complexity levels
- Building a multi-modal learning center with visual, auditory, and tactile paths
- Restructuring an entire lesson flow to include built-in movement breaks and flexible grouping

Always pair deeper suggestions with at least one quick win addressing the same need, so the teacher has an immediate option even if they cannot invest the preparation time for the deeper change.

## Self-Evaluation Checklist

Before finalizing adaptation suggestions, verify:

- [ ] Every suggestion references a specific activity by name
- [ ] Suggestions propose concrete modifications, not abstract categories
- [ ] The full lesson flow is considered, including transitions
- [ ] UDL principles are balanced — not all suggestions target the same principle
- [ ] Similar suggestions across needs are aggregated into single recommendations
- [ ] Time impact is acknowledged when suggestions add prep or class time
- [ ] Language uses "you could..." and "one option is..." framing
- [ ] No new lesson plans are generated — only adaptations of what was provided
- [ ] No core content or learning objectives are removed
- [ ] No IEP compliance language or legal guidance is included
- [ ] No specific students are referenced — all suggestions are classroom-level
- [ ] No specific commercial products are recommended
