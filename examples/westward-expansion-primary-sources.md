# Westward Expansion Through Primary Sources

**Grade**: 7th ELA / Social Studies (Interdisciplinary)
**Block Length**: 45 minutes
**Objective**: Students will analyze primary source documents from the Westward Expansion era and construct a claim about the motivations of settlers using textual evidence.
**Standards**: CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RH.6-8.1, CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RH.6-8.6
**Materials**: Document packet (3 primary sources), annotation guide, exit ticket

---

## Warm-Up (5 minutes)

Display a photograph of a wagon train on the projector. Students do a 2-minute silent "See-Think-Wonder" in their notebooks. They write what they see, what they think is happening, and what they wonder about. After 2 minutes, ask 3-4 students to share their "wonder" questions aloud.

## Vocabulary Introduction (7 minutes)

Introduce 6 key terms students will encounter in the primary sources: manifest destiny, homestead, emigrant, provision, territorial, indigenous. For each word, display the definition on a slide and give one example sentence. Students copy the word and definition into the vocabulary section of their notebooks. Cold-call 2-3 students to use a word in their own sentence.

## Document Analysis — Guided (10 minutes)

Hand out the document packet. The first document is a letter from a woman traveling the Oregon Trail in 1847. Read the first two paragraphs aloud as a class. Model the annotation process on the projector: underline key claims, circle unfamiliar words, write margin notes about the author's perspective. Students follow along and annotate their own copies.

Then ask: "What is this woman's reason for heading west? How do you know?" Take 3-4 responses and chart them on the board under the heading "Motivations."

## Document Analysis — Independent (10 minutes)

Students read Documents 2 and 3 independently. Document 2 is a newspaper advertisement recruiting settlers to Kansas Territory (1855). Document 3 is a diary entry from a 14-year-old boy whose family left Missouri in 1849.

Students annotate both documents using the same method modeled earlier. For each document, they must identify at least one motivation and write it in the margin with the supporting evidence underlined.

Circulate and check annotations. If students finish early, they can compare their annotations with a shoulder partner.

## Group Discussion (8 minutes)

Students form groups of 4. Each group gets a large piece of chart paper divided into three columns, one per document. Groups discuss: what motivated each person/group to move west? They write the motivations and evidence in the appropriate columns.

Each group selects a spokesperson to share their strongest piece of evidence with the class. Chart the responses on the class "Motivations" board from earlier.

## Exit Ticket (5 minutes)

Students return to their seats. On a half-sheet of paper, they respond to the following prompt:

> Based on the documents you read today, what was the most common motivation for settlers moving west? Support your answer with evidence from at least two of the three documents. Write a complete paragraph (claim, evidence, reasoning).

Collect exit tickets at the door.
