# Analyzing Figurative Language in Poetry

**Grade**: 7th ELA
**Block Length**: 50 minutes
**Learning Objective**: Students will identify and analyze figurative language (simile, metaphor, personification, hyperbole, alliteration, imagery, onomatopoeia, idiom) in poetry and explain how it contributes to the poem's meaning and tone.

**Materials**: Projector, whiteboard, printed copies of Poem C (one per student), lined paper, pencils

---

## Vocabulary Review (5 minutes)

Project the following terms and definitions on the board. Students copy all eight into the vocabulary section of their notebooks:

- **Simile**: a comparison between two unlike things using "like" or "as" (e.g., "Her smile was like sunshine")
- **Metaphor**: a direct comparison stating one thing is another (e.g., "Time is a thief")
- **Personification**: giving human qualities to something nonhuman (e.g., "The wind whispered through the trees")
- **Hyperbole**: extreme exaggeration for emphasis (e.g., "I've told you a million times")
- **Alliteration**: repetition of beginning consonant sounds (e.g., "Peter Piper picked a peck")
- **Imagery**: descriptive language that appeals to the senses (e.g., "The warm, golden light spilled across the wooden floor")
- **Onomatopoeia**: a word that imitates a sound (e.g., "buzz," "crash," "sizzle")
- **Idiom**: a phrase whose meaning differs from the literal meaning of the words (e.g., "break a leg")

Students should have all eight definitions recorded before we move on.

## Mini-Lesson: Figurative Language in Action (12 minutes)

Read Robert Frost's "The Road Not Taken" aloud to the class once, all the way through, without stopping. Then project the full text of the poem on the board.

Walk through the poem stanza by stanza, pointing out examples of figurative language as you go:

- Stanza 1: The "two roads diverged in a yellow wood" — metaphor (the roads represent life choices). Imagery in "yellow wood" (visual, appeals to sight).
- Stanza 2: "Had worn them really about the same" — note the contrast between what the speaker claims and what he observes. Not figurative language per se, but sets up the metaphor.
- Stanza 3: "Oh, I kept the first for another day!" — the exclamation and tone. Discuss how the speaker knows he probably won't come back.
- Stanza 4: "I took the one less traveled by, / And that has made all the difference" — the central metaphor extended. The entire poem is an extended metaphor for decision-making.

Students should be taking notes during this walkthrough. Emphasize that figurative language isn't decoration — it's how the poet communicates deeper meaning.

## Guided Practice: Identifying Together (10 minutes)

Project Maya Angelou's "Life Doesn't Frighten Me" (first two stanzas) on the board. Read it aloud once.

Ask students to raise their hands when they spot figurative language. As students identify examples, annotate the poem on the board:

- Circle the figurative language in the text
- Write the type next to it (simile, metaphor, personification, etc.)
- Briefly discuss the effect: What does this figurative language make the reader feel or understand?

Work through both stanzas this way. Try to get through at least 4-5 examples. If students are stuck, prompt with questions like "What is being compared here?" or "Is this meant literally?"

## Independent Analysis (15 minutes)

Hand out printed copies of Langston Hughes's "Dreams." Students read the poem independently and write a 2-3 paragraph response that:

1. Identifies at least 3 examples of figurative language in the poem
2. Names the type of figurative language for each example
3. Explains the effect of each example — how does it contribute to the poem's meaning or tone?

Students should write in complete sentences and use specific quotes from the poem as evidence. Responses will be collected at the end of class.

Remind students to use the vocabulary definitions in their notebooks if they need to check which type of figurative language they're looking at.

## Share Out (5 minutes)

Ask for volunteers to read one of their examples and their analysis aloud to the class. After each volunteer shares, provide brief feedback on their identification and analysis. Try to hear from 3-4 students if time allows.

## Exit Ticket (3 minutes)

On a separate slip of paper, students write one sentence answering this question:

> In "Dreams," Hughes writes "Life is a broken-winged bird that cannot fly." How does this metaphor affect the reader's understanding of the poem's message?

Collect exit tickets at the door as students leave.
