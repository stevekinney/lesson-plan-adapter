## Teaching Context Interpretation Guide

Use the teacher's teaching context to calibrate adaptation suggestions. If a field is not provided, do not assume a value — generate suggestions that work broadly.

### Grade Range

- **K-2**: Favor visual, tactile, and movement-based strategies. Minimize text-heavy modifications. Assume limited independent reading ability. Attention spans are typically 10-15 minutes; plan for frequent transitions.
- **3-5**: Balance visual and text-based strategies. Students can follow multi-step written directions with support. Attention spans are typically 15-20 minutes. Peer-based strategies are emerging.
- **6-8**: Students can engage with more complex text modifications. Peer-based strategies (think-pair-share, peer review, collaborative groups) are particularly effective. Social dynamics matter — avoid strategies that single students out.
- **9-12**: Students can benefit from self-advocacy strategies and metacognitive supports. Technology-based modifications are more practical. Consider the stakes of assessments (grades, credit, graduation).

### Subject Areas

- **ELA**: Text simplification and bilingual support are high-impact. Alternative expression formats (verbal, visual, dramatic) are especially relevant for writing tasks. Reading comprehension benefits heavily from graphic organizers and pre-teaching vocabulary.
- **Math**: Concrete manipulatives and visual representations are high-impact. Reduce quantity of practice problems rather than difficulty level. Allow formula sheets and reference materials. Consider that word problems combine reading and math challenges.
- **Science**: Lab activities already provide multi-modal engagement — focus on adapting lab reports, written analysis, and pre-lab instructions. Safety considerations may limit some physical modifications during experiments.
- **Social Studies**: Primary source analysis benefits heavily from text simplification and graphic organizers. Discussion-based activities offer natural engagement variation. Maps and timelines provide built-in visual supports.
- **Elective**: Elective courses often already have built-in choice and multiple expression modes. Focus on pacing, accessibility of instructional materials, and ensuring equitable participation in hands-on activities.

### Block Length

- **Under 45 minutes**: Avoid suggesting additions that consume more than 5 minutes of instructional time. Focus on modifications to existing activities rather than adding new ones. Every minute counts.
- **45-60 minutes**: Standard planning window. Include one natural break point or transition opportunity mid-block.
- **Over 60 minutes**: Strongly suggest built-in transition and break opportunities. Station rotation becomes more viable. Consider energy management across the extended period.

### Device Availability

- **Students have devices**: Speech-to-text, digital graphic organizers, text-to-speech, recorded responses, and online collaboration tools become viable quick wins.
- **Students do not have devices**: Focus on paper-based modifications. Do not suggest speech-to-text, digital tools, or online resources as quick wins. If suggesting a technology-based strategy, flag that it requires devices the teacher indicated are not available.

### Teaching Priorities

When a teacher provides teaching priorities (e.g., "I prioritize student independence" or "equity is my top concern"), use them to weight and rank suggestions:

- **Weight toward stated values**: If two adaptations are equally viable, prefer the one that aligns with the teacher's stated priority. For example, if the teacher prioritizes student independence, favor self-paced graphic organizers over teacher-led scaffolding.
- **Surface the connection**: When a suggestion aligns with a stated priority, note it briefly in the rationale — "This supports your priority of building student independence by..."
- **Don't filter exclusively**: Priorities are preferences, not hard requirements. Still suggest high-impact adaptations that don't directly align with stated priorities, but rank them lower.
- **If priorities conflict with needs**: When a teaching priority seems to conflict with a learning need (e.g., "I prioritize independence" but a student needs frequent check-ins), acknowledge the tension and suggest a middle path.

### Known Constraints

When a teacher provides known constraints (e.g., "No para support this year" or "30 students, no aide"), treat them as hard limits on what is feasible:

- **Eliminate infeasible suggestions**: Do not suggest adaptations that require resources the teacher has explicitly said are unavailable. If a small-group pull-out requires a paraprofessional and the teacher has none, do not suggest it.
- **Flag constraint-dependent suggestions**: If a suggestion would be ideal but bumps against a constraint, note the constraint and offer a feasible alternative — "Ideally this would use a co-teacher for the small group, but since you noted no para support, consider..."
- **Respect time and staffing constraints**: Constraints about class size, aide availability, and prep time directly affect which effort levels are realistic. A teacher with 30 students and no aide may not be able to implement "deeper" adaptations that require individualized attention.
- **Don't repeat the constraint back unnecessarily**: Reference constraints only when they directly affect a specific suggestion, not as a preamble to every adaptation.
